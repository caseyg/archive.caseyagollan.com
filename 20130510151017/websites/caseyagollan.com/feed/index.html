Yet the proposed mediation was fraught from the very start with problems:

Asymmetry

According to the mediators, the administration initiated this proposed mediation by shopping around within the mediation community for somebody to work with us. The administration chose two mediators and met with them at least twice before anybody involved consulted the students. This goes against the mediators‚Äô own principle of keeping their communications with both parties symmetrical, e.g. after speaking with one party always speak with the other. As the administration has agreed to foot the bill for the proceedings, the administration would also effectively be hiring the mediators. With no insult intended to the mediators‚Äô professionalism, these factors call into question claims of ‚Äúimpartiality‚Äù.

The Cooper community wants to be the administration, not interface with it. Why should it cost $225 for students to speak with already overcompensated administrators? A lot of people have approached me up in arms about shutting up unless I've got a financial solution. I'm of the opinion that financial solutions with any integrity at all will only happen in conjunction with other types of more important changes: systemic, rhetorical, technical.

So here is something nerdy:

This repository contains an archive of Cooper Union's Bylaws from 1996 - 2012. Having done nothing more than pull these documents together into a folder, they already highlight the sloppy and outmoded manner in which Cooper's administrators create, distribute, and archive information.

Going forward I hope to use these documents as raw-material for experiments outlining best practices for living documents and content management systems that are transparent and accountable.

If this collection doesn't look particularly interesting that's because it actually isn't! But getting access to this ostensibly public document's history has been an arduous, bottlenecked procedure (it shouldn't have to be)...and the collection isn't even complete. Where, for example, are the original 1972 Bylaws which all these revisions amend? Where is the history of every document? If we weren't keeping it already why aren't we starting this second?

After a month of heckling the Secretary to the Board of Trustees, a mixed-bag of documents was passed to the college's Library, whose staff quickly posted them online. The files varied substantially in formatting and accessibility and were therefore hard to compare. I set about standardizing the documents as much as possible so the ways that this governing text has changed over time can begin to surface.

The first step of this is that most of the PDFs have been converted by hand into Markdown, a plain-text writing format that provides for a little bit of structure. If you're adventurous, you can already download the files in the /markdown directory and plug them into a diff tool like Kaleidoscope.

In my dream-world I use this space to build tools that shoehorn these principles directly into the administrative workflows responsible for creating and distributing this mess, so that ridiculous retroactive unpacking efforts such as this are not necessary.

But this is a start.

I took an Instagram of a passage about progress in photography to give everyone who ever wrote about progress in photography a spin in their grave.

Nelson and Bush seem to get pretty hung up on technical (or even mechanical) hurdles rather than conceptual ones. There's a lot of fussing about, in Bush's case, how to shuffle microfilm around quickly, or in Nelson's case, complicated server configurations. It reminds me of how characters in sci-fi movies park their hovercars to go use a payphone. These inventors are willing to imagine radically different worlds but can't let go of the most banal limitations. And the things they lamented not having are no longer pipe dreams! Reading their texts in 2012, there appears to be no reason why a Memex or Xanadu can't exist, other than that they just don't.

It seems like Nelson specficially, who I guess is still working, is too smart for his own good. Too wrapped up in the details of his obsessions. "It seemed so simple and clear to me then. It still does," he writes, "But...I mistook a clear view for a short distance." If perfectionism can be said to plague Nelson's projects, it must also be acknowledged that it's his philosophy of choice. I was shocked to read his justification for why Xanadu must be built from scratch, completely and perfectly: "Existing systems do not combine well; hooking them together creates something like the New York subway system."

The New York Subway system?!

In my System Design class, we lauded this as one of the most functional examples of emergent design ever. New York could've suffered from some clunky, overdetermined, shortsighted, top-down transportation plan. Instead, a number of transportation companies competed to get the people where they needed to go. Competition between companies and the lack of an overall design vision led to a shift in emphasis: not what makes sense, but what works well. Perhaps the problems that bogged Nelson down indefinitely only reveal themselves in time, but I wonder if somebody with more distance or a less stubborn idea of the right way to build things could actually build the thing ‚Äî even if it isn't perfect.

I also never realized that Bush thought a lot more about interfaces than Nelson, who basically rejected them entirely (at least as far as I've read):

&gt; How you will look at this world when it is spreadeagled on your screen is your own business: you control it by your choice of screen hardware, by your choice of viewing program, by what you do as you watch, but the structure of the world‚Äîthe system of interconnections of its stored materials‚Äîis the same from screen to screen, no matter how a given screen may show it.

I think a lot about the ability to have my choice of interfaces to the same materials, which is something users and readers are too often denied these days, as the interface and materials have become intertwined under the ownership of the publisher.

Things like RSS feeds, news APIs, and open content sets give me hope. There's also the whole pop-up industry of Read Later tools, whose dirty work it is to boil down webpages to their content and repurpose it for easy reading. It has proven proven easier, in lieu of a perfect system, to ask for forgiveness rather than cooperation in separating content from presentation and bringing it into your own territory.

iPad magazine apps reside at the other end of the spectrum. While they're an additional, native interface that is sometimes exciting, they present a sort of glum vision: impenetrable, redundant, expensive, flashy, proprietary.

Nelson's decoupling of backend and frontend is pretty profound. It underscores the base-ness of his ideas: he's talking about different structures for writing and thinking, not just presenting plain old content in a style that evokes structure. There is not necessarily a visual difference between these two things but conceptually it is huge.

In the past few years I've gone back and forth manically on believing that better tools will make much of a difference. Can you get more powerful and open-ended that a blank sheet of paper or TextEdit window?

But I've recently come to see the "tools make work better or worse" as a false dichotomy: different tools just make working different, in a way that hopefully exists outside of quantification. Increasing power or efficiency is a lot less interesting to me than inventing a game you play with yourself to change the way you think.

You can do everything in the sketches above with a text-editor: collecting, typing, dragging and dropping to reorder, merging and splitting sentences. But a different interface would encourage a different mindset. Different relationships. Different ways of describing what you're doing, like maybe: growing a text instead of writing it. They key actions being: arranging things on a 2D canvas for consideration, and combining related things.

It's almost an architectural conceit: altering the way you can move through a certain space, and trusting that one's environment has a huge impact on everything you do. It's not a question of allowing you to do something magical that didn't exist before, so much as knocking down a few walls and building a swimming pool, to make one's environment feel more conducive to thinking.

Who knows if it would be helpful or frustrating, or if I would ever actually use it, but it's a way of working that has sat in the back of my mind for a long time and has even surfaced in analog form. If the entrepreneurs behind Code Year (and the masses of users they‚Äôve signed up for lessons) are all hoping to ride the wave of digital change, Wendy Hui Kyong Chun, a professor of Modern Culture and Media at Brown University, is the academic trying to pause for a moment to take stock of the present situation and see where software is actually headed. All the frenzy about apps and ‚Äúthe cloud,‚Äù Chun argues, is just another turn in the ‚Äúcycles of obsolescence and renewal‚Äù that define new media. The real change, which Chun lays out in her book Programmed Visions: Software and Memory, is that ‚Äúprogrammability,‚Äù the logic of computers, has come to reach beyond screens into both the systems of government and economics and the metaphors we use to make sense of the world.

‚ÄúWithout [computers, human and mechanical],‚Äù writes Chun, ‚Äúthere would be no government, no corporations, no schools, no global marketplace, or, at the very least, they would be difficult to operate...Computers, understood as networked software and hardware machines, are‚Äîor perhaps more precisely set the grounds for‚Äîneoliberal governmental technologies...not simply through the problems (population genetics, bioinformatics, nuclear weapons, state welfare, and climate) they make it possible to both pose and solve, but also through their very logos, their embodiment of logic.‚Äù

What's the difference between hardware, software, firmware, and wetware? Source code, compiled code, and written instructions? What is a thing and how did software become one? The term ‚Äúvaporware‚Äù refers to software that‚Äôs announced and advertised but never actually released for use, such as Ted Nelson‚Äôs infamous Xanadu project. The key source of these misunderstandings is the use of the computer as metaphor.

People in previous generations conceptualized the world around them using technologies like clocks and steam engines. While these analog, mechanical devices are intricate, if one were to take apart a clock and and put it back together its inner workings could be understood. Digital computers are more complex because they are made of both tangible chips and immaterial codes, neither of which are intuitive to deconstruct. Recalling Stewart Brand‚Äôs call for a picture of the whole earth in 1968, Chun poses the question: what would a picture of the whole Internet look like? Except, in this case, to find out may not be the point. In the way that the stock market is based on speculation‚Äîvirally spreading fear about the future of a company (as opposed to concrete evidence or actual bad management decisions) can cause a stock to tank‚Äîa technologized world is increasingly based on conjecture. How we got where we are today

Computers don‚Äôt know anything. They have to &bull;be programmed and designed ex&bull;tensively to make sense as tools for accomplishing even the most basic of tasks. If you wanted to draw a circle using an untrained computer, you‚Äôd have to tell the computer what a ‚Äúcircle‚Äù is, how to draw a line, and even that there‚Äôs something on which to draw. Today this is not the case. Tools for accomplishing most tasks have already been written, so you don‚Äôt have to know how to program in order to get things done. Photoshop, for example, makes drawing a circle into a single drag-and-drop action so intuitive that you never have to consider that there are complex procedures and equations going on behind the scenes.

A computer connected to the internet becomes not just a tool for drawing circles, but a tool for communication and a magical container for all the forms of media that preceded it. The major websites on which artists have come to work and play: Facebook, Tumblr, YouTube, and Twitter, each do something a little different, but they are all free, mass-market, ‚ÄúWeb 2.0‚Äù tools, intended to connect users to each other through content they contribute: profiles, posts, videos, status updates. All types of people use these sites as entertainment and ways to keep up with friends and family, but for artists social networks also function as a casual and surprisingly robust publishing and distribution system, and are sometimes the subject of the work itself.

For the computer to become so widely used as a live, work and play space, it had to be modeled largely after the life which preceded it. On his Post Internet blog Gene McHugh writes, ‚Äú[the] ‚ÄòInternet‚Äô became not a thing in the world to escape into, but rather the world one sought escape from‚Ä¶sigh‚Ä¶It became the place where business was conducted, and bills were paid. It became the place where people tracked you down.‚Äù Likewise, everyday life has become technologized to the extent that offline interactions now occur in the language of technology. A good conversation puts us ‚Äúon the same wavelength,‚Äù as if we‚Äôre radios. The word ‚Äúlike‚Äù is becoming primarily a verb used to describe the action of ‚Äúliking‚Äù a Facebook page.

It‚Äôs easy to forget that Facebook didn‚Äôt exist until 2004, YouTube 2005, Twitter 2006, and Tumblr 2007. Networked technology is now present everywhere in everything from our always-on cellphones to connected urban infrastructures to autonomous global stock markets. Our lives have become so integrated with networks in such short order that it is almost unimportant to distinguish between communication on and offline. While my grandpa grins from ear-to-ear each time we video chat and talks about how we‚Äôre living in the future, my little sister routinely hangs out with her friends online, leaving the video chat window open in the background as a kind of presence. The perpetual connectedness of rising generations has kicked the computer‚Äôs status as spectacle into rapid decline.

John Durham Peters, who ponders ‚Äúbeing on the same wavelength‚Äù in his book Speaking into the Air: A History of the Idea of Communication, writes that, ‚Äúcommunication as a person-to-person activity became thinkable only in the shadow of mediated communication.‚Äù He writes in the past tense with a certain sense of finality, as if this shift happened at a certain point in time and never changed. But new tools are being introduced every day, and with each system update or new website we sign up for the kinds of mediation we engage with change. It‚Äôs not so much that technology has crept into everyday life but that there is a back-and-forth exchange of metaphor between online and off; a continuous push and pull between fashioning our tools and being shaped by them.

How our tools have changed

In 1985, Marvin Minsky published The Society of Mind, a book that attempts to explain the possibility of artificial intelligence by atomizing complex human activities. For example, ‚Äúmaking something out of blocks‚Äù is broken down into a tree of yes or no actions called ‚Äúagents.‚Äù Minsky‚Äôs reductionism offers a remarkably clear description of how we interact with technology:

When you drive a car, you regard the steering wheel as an agency that you can use to change the car‚Äôs direction. You don‚Äôt care how it works. But when something goes wrong with the steering, and you want to understand what‚Äôs happening, it‚Äôs better to regard the steering wheel as just one agent in a larger agency: it turns a shaft that turns a gear to pull a rod that shifts the axle of a wheel. Of course, one doesn‚Äôt always want to take this microscopic view; if you kept all those details in mind while driving, you might crash because it took too long to figure out which way to turn the wheel. Knowing how is not the same as knowing why.

Minsky‚Äôs distinction between having the ability to use a tool and understanding how it works is even more important today than it was in the 80‚Äôs because technology has become better, closer, and harder to distinguish, though it would appear that nothing has changed. One reason it‚Äôs easy to get confused is that designers attempt to hide when systems are technologized.

Several years ago when my mom‚Äôs car stalled on the highway, the repairman told her that the car was physically in fine shape but that its internal computer‚Äîan intermediary between the pedals and engine that serves as a sort of control center‚Äîhad failed. A specialized code repairman had to be called in to run a diagnostic and figure out what went wrong.

When Minsky wrote about cars the underlying system was only hidden behind a facade of metal and plastic. The rods and gears between steering wheel and axle could be taken apart piece-by-piece and put back together in order to understand how the car works. Computers allow for an interface, like pedals or a steering wheel, to be decoupled from its mechanics. In a computerized car, changes to the steering wheel still result in a shifting of the axle but these actions are triggered by code, sensors, chips and wires (or even wireless signals). Try taking apart a microchip. Not only is it hard to reverse engineer without specialized knowledge but companies often intentionally block their users from accessing their proprietary code. If we‚Äôre not granted access to the obfuscated code loaded onto our cars‚Äô computer chips, we can no longer really take our cars apart and put them back together again, and we have lost the potential to understand yet another one of the systems that govern our lives.

When we can‚Äôt deal with technology on its own terms: code, we rely on the metaphors presented by its interface to come up with a story about how it works. My mom didn‚Äôt even know her car had a computer in it until it broke down because it looked and worked just like every other vehicle she had ever owned. With a bit of cleverness, programmers and designers are able to make a computerized car appear no more complicated than a car from the 80‚Äôs though a computer is invisibly routing input from the steering wheel to the axles and optimizing for fuel efficiency. Because of this kind of hiding at both the interface and code levels, our attempts at understanding the tools we work with today more often result in amassing a series of built-up misunderstandings. The car is an example of a relatable system that is secretly complex, but we also tend to project a sense of complexity onto simple systems. Paul Ford‚Äôs essay Time‚Äôs Inverted Index illustrates the way we filter a computer‚Äôs indifferent output, like the results of a keyword search covering ten years of email, through our emotional brains:

It is not the fault of the software, which is by definition unwitting. No one writing code said, ‚ÄúLet‚Äôs totally mess with his perception of self and understanding of free will.‚Äù The code sees an email that is filled with words as something like a small bucket that is filled with coins. It takes the coins out one by one and stacks them in squares marked out for each denomination, adding a slip of paper between each coin that indicates the coin‚Äôs bucket of origin. Then it does the same with the next bucket. There are many denominations of coins; a truly big table, called an inverted index, is needed to hold them all. The coin-sorting program cannot think or advise, but it does allow you to go to a pile of coins on the table, pick them up, and know their buckets of origin. It‚Äôs my unrigid brain that turns a sorted stack of coins into a story‚Äîso there I was, querying against my life, tapping the touchscreens of fate and clicking the mouse of destiny, all of it suffused with a sort of sweet nostalgia. (Of my emails, 893 mention libraries; 311 mention libraries and love.)

Putting aside early experiments in Information Retrieval, there was really no such thing as ‚Äúsearch,‚Äù as we know it, before web search was put into practical use in the 90‚Äôs5. Yet search has become a paradigm. A fundamental way we expect to access information. An inalienable right. I searched Google 46 times today alone. If we‚Äôre not looking critically at the way technology orders our experience, we‚Äôre operating on juxtapositions of information which may be arbitrary, unimportant, or‚Äîas is the case with the computerized car‚Äîbeyond understanding. On top of this we interpret these results as bearing emotion, utility and truth.

Google is working hard to maintain the feeling of simplicity and effortlessness while simultaneously increasing the underlying complexity that makes results better. A promotional site boasts, ‚Äúat any given time there are 50-200 different versions of our core algorithm out in the wild. Millions more when you realize your search results are personalized to you and you alone.‚Äù There is still only one search box, but there are as many different result sets as there are visitors to Google‚Äôs site. While my mom misunderstood her car, she at least had a mental model of cars from before computers to believe in. Software with no real life counterpart can be tricky because all we have to go on in terms of creating our stories is an abstract, utilitarian interface like a textbox, search button, and perhaps some basic ideas about servers sending bits to and fro. The same clever design that makes complicated software easy to use makes it hard to truly understand. This confusion can even turn into hysteria as people in a thousand-comment long discussion try blindly to decipher the hidden meanings in the way their friends are ordered on Facebook.

Abstract inventions like search boxes also find their way back into real life in curious ways. I find myself strangely frustrated in offline situations that lack Google‚Äôs utility, like a used bookstore with no searchable catalog. But Google has not just changed expectations about the findability of information, it has shaped the way that we ask for it. We used to ask questions of a librarian or an expert, but computers are not yet adept at processing ‚Äúnatural language‚Äù so we meet them halfway by reducing our questions to queries composed of keywords and operators. These changes reverberate around the world, and it happens fast. When Google tweaked their interface to instantly show results before users even finish typing a query, half-typed words and phrases immediately started to show up in the referral logs of my website. The progression from posing questions, to assembling queries, to not even typing out half a query, shows that the ways our computers shape us are plainly visible.

Life in the shadow of technology

In their paper Bias in Computer Systems researchers Batya Friedman and Helen Nissenbaum examine the ways in which, ‚Äúgroups or individuals can be systematically and unfairly discriminated against in favor of others by computer systems.‚Äù

Computer systems‚Ä¶are comparatively inexpensive to disseminate, and thus, once developed, a biased system has the potential for widespread impact. If the system becomes a standard in the field, the bias becomes pervasive. If the system is complex, and most are, biases can remain hidden in the code, difficult to pinpoint or explicate, and not necessarily disclosed to users or their clients. Furthermore, unlike in our dealings with biased individuals with whom a potential victim can negotiate, biased systems offer no equivalent means for appeal.

Even when users are not being intentionally manipulated, there is an opportunity cost of docilely operating within dominant software patterns. Of the three types of bias the authors have identified, we have already encountered two: Preexisting Bias and Technical Bias. ‚ÄúPreexisting Bias has its roots in social institutions, practices, and attitudes.‚Äù The computer environment described by McHugh in which we conduct business and pay bills, that literally mimics real world drudgery with its office-like files and folders, is just one example of how interface metaphors can carry not just visual but social conventions from the physical into the digital realm.

While Friedman and Nissenbaum note that almost all instances of computer bias can be classified as either Preexisting or Technical, they identify a third type called Emergent Bias, which is particularly important for artists working with Web 2.0 tools. Emergent bias arises ‚Äúafter a design is completed, as a result of a change in the context or usage of a system.‚Äù The hacking and retrofitting of existing software in the service of creative expression or critique is exciting, but assumptions of the software might still be carried by the work. Friedman and Nissenbaum write that any time the way a user wants to work with a system butt up against its constraints, and the work they are doing is disadvantaged as a result, it can be classified as a case of Emergent Bias called ‚Äúuser mismatch.‚Äù Unlike victims of bias, however, artists tend to seek out these problematic areas.

In Ann Hirsch‚Äôs project Scandalishious, she satirizes the ‚Äúcamwhore‚Äù culture of YouTube using YouTube itself, by taking on a character named Caroline and posting videos of her own. A site dedicated to user generated content can spawn a culture of typical users as well as a group that is critical of them, so it‚Äôs hard to tell if using a site to critique how people use a site stretches what is considered a ‚Äútypical‚Äù use of the site. While the videos are funny, Hirsch has also published serious writing on this topic, such as her brief essay, Women, Sexuality, and The Internet. However, Hirsch‚Äôs work and others like it may actually be strongest at the points where it becomes unclear if the artist is omnisciently addressing big problems and questions or enjoying being a part of the scene.

On platforms such as YouTube neither the ‚Äútypical‚Äù cultures that arise nor the people who examine and critique those cultures are disadvantaged by design, because the companies behind Web 2.0 tools are simply providing free web services to all.

Artists attempting to be critical of our relationship to social networking sites should follow the money back to advertising, where they will find irony in embedding themselves within the systems they seek to explicate. In You Are Not a Gadget, Jaron Lanier writes:

The customers of social networks are not the members of those networks‚Ä¶the whole idea of fake friendship, is just bait laid by the lords of the clouds to lure hypothetical advertisers‚Ä¶who could someday show up‚Ä¶If the revenue never appears, then a weird imposition of a database-as-reality ideology will have colored generations of teen peer group and romantic experiences for no business or other purpose.

Tumblr, the only one of the four major tools mentioned that does not heavily plaster content pages with display advertising, appears to be taking a financial loss and burning through venture capital money to grow their userbase and integrate their product more deeply into users‚Äô lives. Starting a conversation about how computers shape our lives is important, but responses that don‚Äôt functionally uproot those systems will have bizarrely turned artists working with the internet today into a product whose criticality is being sold back to advertisers.

In defense of building tools

Is it possible to make pointed critiques by changing platforms rather than contributing to them? In Bless the Toolmakers, Robin Sloan argues that producing content is a more resonant form of communication than building tools:

I don‚Äôt think about Steve Jobs when I‚Äôm using my MacBook. I don‚Äôt think about Thomas Knoll when I‚Äôm using Photoshop. I don‚Äôt think about the sublime inventor of the kitchen table (her name lost to history) when I sit here at mine. (I don‚Äôt think about the Ikea designer who made this particular model, either.)

While the author may be more present in content than platform, the importance of addressing technology on the level of its underlying systems cannot be overstated. It doesn‚Äôt matter how critical what you‚Äôre saying is, the companies who thrive on user generated content would like very much for you to continue generating it at length. Content is an icky word for a reason: it doesn‚Äôt make any distinction between what is being said or to whom; it is something measured in click-throughs and conversion rates rather than poignancy or intelligence. In Marshall McLuhan‚Äôs famous words, ‚Äúthe medium is the message.‚Äù Power lies with the makers of mediums, or to use terms less slippery within an art context: tools and platforms. Though design patterns, behaviors, and world views can swiftly circulate between the real and the virtual, it is rare to see users‚Äîartists especially‚Äîbeing the ones to create tools directly.

Creating new systems means anticipating the full extent of biases and very intentionally keeping or discarding them. There can be no compromise. Even a tiny adjustment to the way an existing system introduces bias might necessitate throwing out everything on which it is built and starting from scratch. Tasking artists with building alternative systems for digital existence may seem overly pragmatic. In addition to the work it takes to dissect the ways computers warp our relationships with ourselves and each other, creating tools that address this requires experience with code. However, organizations like Creativetime have been pushing hard at the idea of ‚Äúuseful art‚Äù that seem to be nothing more than a thinly veiled draft of artists into the humanitarian workforce. It‚Äôs not in so-called useful art, but on the internet where platforms, ‚Äúblur the forms of art and everyday life, and emphasize participation, dialogue, and community engagement.‚Äù Experiments with technology have the potential to go beyond the symbolism of protest, the red tape of politicians, and the bureaucracy of non-profit organizations in their potential to create change. ‚Äú[Technologists] tinker with your philosophy by direct manipulation of your cognitive experience, not indirectly, through argument. It takes only a tiny group of engineers to create technology that can shape the entire future of human experience with incredible speed,‚Äù writes Jaron Lanier.

Journalism has been one of the more progressive fields in terms of adopting custom-made tools and interfaces. Perhaps this is because, in the words of Clay Shirky, newspapers are seeing, ‚Äúold stuff get broken faster than the new stuff is put in its place.‚Äù In a blog post titled In Defense of Building Tools, Derek Willis, a developer for the New York Times, writes:

A good tool doesn‚Äôt just make it easier for a reporter to create a story. It actually seeds the story, or makes it possible for more people in a newsroom to collaborate. When you have data but no tool, you become a gatekeeper of a sorts ‚Äì which is appropriate in many circumstances, but not all. I can‚Äôt possibly know what my colleagues are thinking about, considering or being alerted to, but I can make it easier for them to test out theories and do some exploration on their own. Some of them prefer to do their own work, and we certainly miss some opportunities for apps that way. But others consult with me quite a bit, since they now have a much better idea of what we have and what we might be able to do with it.

Willis‚Äôs examples are tailored towards journalism but his logic applies soundly to artists working with the internet too. Tools have the potential to exponentially improve the ability for collaboration and understanding. In one interesting experiment from the New York Times, comment threads for highly discussed articles are been elegantly improved by integrating hard data from users that allows thousands of responses to be mapped onto a zeitgeist visualizing matrix.

The authors of a book called Newsgames even pose that journalism has a lot to learn from videogames! As a rich experience that cannot exist on any previous medium alone, the authors propose that so called ‚Äúnewsgames‚Äù could teach people the dynamics of complicated systems through simulated first-hand experience, rather than by inference from an account of events or by reading dry explanations. Newspapers and other ‚Äúold media‚Äù employ a paid content publishing model, as opposed to relying on the explosion of free user generated content. Even in the supplemental role that the authors suggest newsgames might play, their vision is impressive for how it looks beyond just content to systems.

Knowing how even the mundane aspects of computing like files and folders can filter back into the way we think and behave, what if we redesigned these utilitarian aspects of computing to incorporate more inventive modes of working? Planetary is an iPad app by a company called Bloom which visualizes a user‚Äôs iTunes library in the form of a galaxy rather than a spreadsheet. While at first I was put off by how superfluous this seems, the designers believe ‚Äúthese Instruments aren‚Äôt merely games or graphics. They‚Äôre new ways of seeing what‚Äôs important.‚Äù What if instead of files and folders, we visualized what we‚Äôre working on like a solar system? Ideas could be set into orbit at different speeds, which would determine how which things appear together, rather than folder hierarchies. I often find myself navigating a crowded array of overlapping windows, which seems symptomatic of multitasking or ADD, so how would a system that takes this mindset as a starting point ideally work?

These bespoke systems don‚Äôt have to save the world. What did files and folders ever do for you? Lanier seem to hint that popular interfaces become pervasive more by conspiracy or agressive promotion than on merit anyway. Simply introducing different kinds of arbitrariness may lead to accidental discoveries about the way we work and live.

In addition to new interfaces, the ‚Äúfront-end‚Äù of a system, there is also the possibility of creating radical new back-ends. These low level designs, while less visually compelling, they are conceptualy mindblowing. Wired magazine has called Xanadu, a system proposed by Ted Nelson (inventor of the word ‚Äúhypertext‚Äù18) both, ‚Äúthe most radical computer dream of the hacker era,‚Äù and, ‚Äúthe longest-running vaporware project in the history of computing.‚Äù Xanadu assumes an entirely different relationship with ‚Äúcontent‚Äù than we are used to. The web, as we know it, is made up of documents which link to each other. Xanadu‚Äôs key difference is it‚Äôs rigid structure, in which all links must be bi-directional. For example, if you wanted to take a piece of one document and put it into another, instead of ‚Äúcopy and pasting‚Äù you would do something called ‚Äútranscluding.‚Äù While on a functional level copy and pasting takes a series of characters in one document and replicates them in another, transclusion less redundantly embeds a linked version of the original document into the new one. Good design might make the interaction of transcluding no more complicated than a copy and paste, but transclusions are more meaningful because they can be followed like links from use to use. If the author of the transcluded fragment had taken it from somewhere else, a user could continue to follow the phrase through all of its uses. Xanadu would create radically different possibilities and limitations in both digital and physical space. In fact, Nelson envisioned that Xanadu‚Äôs linked attribution could be tied into a micropayment system that would end copyright disputes and provide content creators with steady income unlike the theories of ‚Äúfree‚Äù which have popped up to explain our present situation. A collage of images made with source material from ten different people would be traceable back to each of them and payments as small as a few cents could trickle back to the image owners for every single use. In the bi-directionally linked world of Xanadu, the metaphor of files and folders becomes unimportant. Nelson evocatively describes flying through documents in 3D gaming space. With the continuous exchange of metaphors between screen and life, de-emphasizing hierarchies of files and folders on screen might open up the way we think when we‚Äôre away from screens too. Such specific systems may never be of use to larges sets of people, but experiments with more opinionated software are an exciting prospect.

The way things are now is more like a pile up of metaphors and recycled code than laws of interaction which are set in stone. As can be seen in the examples above, designing computer systems is an unprecedentedly fast and direct way of altering how people experience the world and relate to each other. In the coming years, as code literacy becomes more important, artists will be empowered to create new platforms with the conceptual backbone lacking in today‚Äôs popular offerings. Artists who are already thinking critically about the way networked technology orders our experience should go beyond generating content to become makers of mediums, if only so that whatever comes after Web 2.0 offers more to artists than free hosting of its own critique.

If Van den Dorpel&#8217;s non-linear relational archive is imagined as it could exist in physical space it would work like this: from a curtain behind which hundreds of objects are stored, one object is brought out and placed on a stage, flanked by photographs of several related objects. While this system works in real life, it is not fast, efficient or practical in the ways virtual space and relational databases afford.

To allow the user of the physical archive to ask questions of the assistants shuffling objects and images back and forth, for example, &#8220;how do you choose the relevant items?&#8221; would quickly reveal the system&#8217;s inner workings. The other option would be to brute force the system, requesting each object in numerical order. Perhaps with Van den Dorpel&#8217;s archive, the intent is to direct the users to a specific experience or understanding without revealing the system&#8217;s coarse logic. But Swinehart&#8217;s method of reverse-engineering: working outside of the suggested navigation, taking thorough notes, and charting the results, presents an alternative kind of understanding.
